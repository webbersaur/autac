Sourcing Retractile & Coiled Cords in Indiana: Industries, Lead Times & Supplier Criteria

A practical guide for Indiana procurement teams and engineers — which industries buy coiled cordage, why buying direct from a Connecticut manufacturer beats distributors and imports, what lead times to expect, and how to evaluate a supplier.

By Marie-Louise Burkle, CEO Published June 17, 2026 11 min read

Indiana's Manufacturing Base — and Who Buys Coiled Cordage

Indiana is one of the most manufacturing-intensive states in the country by share of GDP, and that density is exactly why retractile, curly, and coiled cords move through so many Indiana plants. If you are responsible for sourcing industrial cordage for an Indiana operation — whether you are a procurement manager qualifying a new approved vendor or an engineer specifying a cord for a new equipment platform — it helps to understand where the demand concentrates and what each cluster actually needs.

Five distinct clusters drive most of the state's appetite for coiled power and signal cords:

  • RV, towable & motorhome manufacturing. Elkhart County is the RV Capital of the World, where roughly 80 percent of North America's recreational vehicles are built. Thor Industries is headquartered in Elkhart and builds Airstream, Heartland, and Keystone product through its subsidiaries; Forest River runs more than 100 plants out of Elkhart; Jayco assembles in Middlebury; and Grand Design RV, Alliance RV, plus tier-one suppliers Lippert and Patrick Industries round out the cluster. Fast-moving assembly lines with frequent floorplan changes need overhead retractile power drops and tool cords that pull cleanly off the line between cycles.
  • Automotive, engines & commercial vehicle. Cummins is headquartered in Columbus, where it designs and tests diesel and natural-gas engines. Subaru of Indiana Automotive assembles in Lafayette, Toyota Motor Manufacturing Indiana builds in Princeton, Honda assembles in Greensburg, and Allison Transmission builds commercial-duty transmissions in Indianapolis. These plants run high-current tool drops, dynamometer and test-cell feeds, and inspection-station cordage that must survive cutting fluids, coolants, and constant flexing.
  • Pharmaceutical, medical device & life sciences. Eli Lilly is headquartered in Indianapolis, Roche Diagnostics operates its North American manufacturing there, Warsaw is the Orthopedic Capital of the World anchored by Zimmer Biomet, and Cook Medical manufactures in Bloomington. Cleanroom controls, sterilization-compatible jackets, and FDA Design History File traceability shape every cord spec.
  • Aerospace, defense & propulsion. Rolls-Royce designs and tests aircraft and defense engines in Indianapolis, supported by a dense base of machining, plating, and assembly suppliers feeding engine test cells and ground support equipment under AS9100 and ITAR requirements.
  • Steel, heavy industry & metal fabrication. The Calumet Region around Gary and Burns Harbor is one of the largest integrated steelmaking centers in the United States, with Cleveland-Cliffs, U.S. Steel Gary Works, NLMK Indiana, and Steel Dynamics operating mills and finishing lines that punish ordinary cordage with heat, oil, scale, and abrasion.

Each cluster carries its own engineering requirements — current rating, jacket compound, flex life, shielding, and environmental exposure. The common thread is that all five buy coiled cordage in volume, and all five care about supplier quality and supply chain resilience. Why Buy Direct From a Connecticut Manufacturer Instead of a Distributor or Importer

Indiana buyers have three broad paths to source coiled cords: a Midwest distributor, an overseas-import supplier, or direct from a domestic manufacturer. Each has tradeoffs, but for OEMs that depend on consistent quality and predictable delivery, buying direct from the factory is usually the strongest position.

Distributors add a layer of markup and a layer of lead time. A distributor stocks a limited range of common configurations and marks them up to cover their margin. When you need a custom cord — a specific gauge, retracted length, or jacket compound — the distributor has to route the request back to the manufacturer anyway, which means you pay more and wait longer for the same product. You also lose direct access to the engineers who actually understand the cord.

Imports look cheaper on the unit price and rarely stay that way. Overseas cordage adds weeks of ocean transit, customs clearance, and broker fees before it ever reaches a Midwest distribution center. UL listings on imported product can be hard to verify, quality control is difficult to manage remotely, and when a problem surfaces after delivery, resolution takes months rather than days. Tariffs and freight erode the price advantage, and a long, opaque supply chain is exactly the kind of risk most procurement teams are now trying to engineer out.

Buying direct from a domestic manufacturer like Autac means factory pricing with no distributor markup, a single point of contact for engineering questions, and a short, transparent, domestic-only supply chain. It also means you can talk to the people who build the cord when you need to change a specification or expedite an order. Autac sells direct to Indiana OEMs and end users from our North Branford, Connecticut factory — no Midwest distributor in the middle.

Supplier-Evaluation Checklist for Indiana Buyers

Whether you are qualifying Autac or comparing several suppliers, use these criteria to make an objective, side-by-side decision. Each one maps to a real procurement or compliance risk.

Criterion What to Verify Why It Matters in Indiana
UL / CUL listing Provide UL file number; verify in UL's database. Mark on the jacket, not just the brochure. Recognized by Indiana OSHA and every Indiana Authority Having Jurisdiction as the baseline electrical safety standard.
Lot traceability Confirm full lot traceability and material-content documentation on request. Required for quality audits, supplier qualification reviews, and FDA DHF submissions.
WBENC / diversity spend Verify current WBENC (Women-Owned Business Enterprise) certification. Counts toward diversity-spend targets common in large Indiana OEM procurement programs.
Custom capability Can they customize gauge, conductor count, length, jacket compound, shielding, and terminations? RV floorplan changes, engine test cells, and device prototypes all need non-standard builds.
Domestic supply chain Where is the cord physically made? Is the supply chain US-only? Simplifies ITAR-program qualification and removes import lead-time and tariff risk.
Quality documentation PPAP/FAI for automotive, AS9100-adjacent for aerospace, FDA DHF support for medical. Lets you integrate the supplier into existing approved-vendor and supplier-survey workflows.
Minimum order quantity Ask the MOQ for both standard and custom orders. No-MOQ builds make it practical to validate a design with a small pilot before scaling.
Longevity & stability How long has the company operated? Any ownership changes? A decades-long track record signals a lower-risk addition to your supply chain.
