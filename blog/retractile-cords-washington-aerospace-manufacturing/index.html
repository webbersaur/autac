Retractile Cords for Washington Aerospace Manufacturing

Inside the Boeing-anchored Puget Sound supplier base, the cord and cable challenges that come with building airplanes, and how UL-listed retractile, curly, and coiled cordage — engineered in Connecticut, shipped to Washington — solves them.

Every one of those operations runs hand tools, fixtures, test benches, and ground support equipment that need durable, traceable power and signal delivery. Why Aerospace Assembly Is Hard on Cords

An aircraft final-assembly line is one of the most demanding environments a power cord will ever see. Tools move constantly along long, complex lines. Operators reach into wings, fuselages, and confined structures, dragging cords behind them thousands of times per shift. A straight cord lying across a work area is a trip hazard, a foreign-object-debris (FOD) risk, and a fast-wearing consumable. The specific stresses that drive cord failure in this setting include:

  • Flex-cycle fatigue. Conductors that flex tens of thousands of times at the same point work-harden and break. Stranding construction and jacket flexibility determine how long a cord survives.
  • Pull and retraction force. A cord that does not retract cleanly drapes across the floor; one that retracts too aggressively yanks tools out of position. Coil memory has to be tuned, not accidental.
  • Current at length. A tool drawing 15A at the plug end loses voltage over a long extended coil. Gauge has to be sized for the load at full extension, not just nameplate amperage.
  • Solvent and fluid exposure. Sealants, hydraulic fluids, cutting fluids, and cleaning solvents attack the wrong jacket compound.
  • EMI in test and integration. Avionics bench-test and electrical-systems integration work generates and is sensitive to electrical noise, which means shielding on signal lines.
  • Traceability. AS9100 and ITAR-program supplier-quality requirements demand lot traceability and a documented, U.S.-based supply chain.

How Retractile and Coiled Cords Answer Those Stresses

A retractile (coiled) cord is engineered specifically to live in this environment. The helical coil stores its own slack: at rest it pulls back out of the work zone, eliminating the trip-and-FOD hazard of a straight cord, and it extends only as far as the operator needs. Because the coil distributes flex across the whole helix rather than at a single bend point, a properly built retractile cord outlasts a straight cord by a wide margin in repetitive-reach applications — exactly the duty cycle of a drill, rivet gun, or fastening tool at a wing or fuselage station.

The engineering levers a manufacturer controls — and that an aerospace buyer should specify — are retracted length, extension ratio, conductor count and gauge, jacket compound, shielding, and termination. Get those right and the cord becomes a tuned tool rather than a generic consumable. The retractile cords hub covers the standard catalog configurations; for high-flex signal work, curly cords use the same coil principle at lighter gauges, and coiled cords cover shielded, multi-conductor builds.

Choosing the Right Jacket Compound

Jacket compound is the single most consequential material decision for an aerospace cord, because it governs solvent resistance, flex life, temperature range, and how the cord ages. Autac runs several compound families, and the right choice depends on the shop environment:

  • TPR (thermoplastic rubber). The workhorse for assembly-line and composite-cell tool drops. Excellent flexibility and coil memory, good abrasion resistance, and clean handling. The default for 12–14 AWG power drops.
  • Auta-Prene (thermoset rubber). Autac's rugged compound for harsh environments — resists oils, solvents, sealants, and rough handling. The choice for GSE, NDT, and any cell with resin or solvent exposure.
  • SJOW / SOW UL types. Oil-, water-, and abrasion-resistant jacketed cord types for service-power and shop-floor drops that see fluids and wash-down.
  • TPE and PVC. Lighter-duty options for benign indoor bench and test environments where solvent exposure is minimal.

For signal and avionics work, the conductor and shield construction matter more than the jacket alone — specify a braided or spiral shield and confirm conductor color coding against your plant standard using the conductor color charts.

Current Ratings, Gauge, and Length: Sizing the Cord to the Load

The most common aerospace cord-sizing mistake is matching gauge to the tool's nameplate amperage without accounting for the extended length of the coil. A retractile cord's working length at full extension is typically four to five times its retracted length, and voltage drop accumulates over that full run. A 14 AWG cord that is fine at 6 feet may sag under a 15A continuous load at 30 feet extended.

The practical rule for the Washington applications above: use 14 AWG, 3-conductor for 15A tools on moderate extensions, step to 12 AWG when the extended length is long or the duty is continuous, and use 10 AWG / 20A for GSE and test-stand loads. For light signal and instrumentation lines, 16–18 AWG shielded constructions carry the data without adding stiffness that hurts coil memory. Specify retracted length and extension ratio together — a 24" retracted cord at a 1:5 ratio gives roughly 10 feet of clean working reach.
